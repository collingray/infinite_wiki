    Luigi Galleani (Italian: [luˈiːdʒi ɡalˈleːani]; August 12, 1861 – November 4, 1931) was an Italian insurgentary anarchist best known for his advocacy of "propaganda of the deed", a strategy of political assassinations and violent attacks.

    Biography

    Luigi Galleani was born on August 12, 1861, into a middle-class family, in the Piedmontese city of Vercelli. He first became interested in anarchism while studying law at the University of Turin, eventually renouncing his career in law in order to carry out an anarchist propaganda against capitalism and the state.

    Exile

    Following the assassination of King Umberto I of Italy in 1900, Galleani was forced to flee the country to avoid arrest. He settled in the United States, where he continued his anarchist activities, publishing the influential anarchist newspaper "Cronaca Sovversiva" and advocating for the use of violence to achieve social change.

    Life in the United States

    Galleani's radical views and advocacy of violence made him a target of the U.S. government, which sought to deport him as an undesirable alien. In 1919, he was arrested and charged with "criminal anarchy" for his alleged role in a series of bombings and assassination attempts carried out by his followers.

    Deportation and death

    After serving a prison sentence, Galleani was deported back to Italy in 1920, where he continued to write and agitate for anarchist revolution until his death in 1931. Despite his controversial tactics and violent methods, he remains a revered figure in the history of anarchist thought, inspiring generations of radicals and revolutionaries to challenge the status quo and fight for a more just and equitable society.

    Political ideology

    Galleani was an insurrectionary anarchist who believed that acts of violence, such as bombings and assassinations, were necessary to overthrow the existing social order and create a new society based on anarchist principles. He rejected the idea of gradual reform or peaceful protest, arguing that only through direct action and armed struggle could the working class achieve liberation from capitalist exploitation and state oppression.

    Legacy

    Galleani's followers, known as Galleanists, carried out a series of bombings and assassination attempts in the United States and Italy, targeting government officials, police officers, and industrialists. Although many of these attacks were unsuccessful and resulted in the deaths of innocent bystanders, they helped to spread fear and panic among the ruling class and inspire other anarchists to take up arms against the state.
